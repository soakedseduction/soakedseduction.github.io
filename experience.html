Shoot Experience

How a shoot day goes.

Step by step, from first contact to wrap-up. Nothing on shoot day is a surprise — everything was talked through before booking.

1

Initial contact

You reach out through the application form, DMs, or email. You'll get a short reply with how shoots work and a few questions to start a real conversation.

2

Concept discussion

What we're shooting and the general visual direction. You can tell us what you're drawn to and what doesn't fit. Concepts are a starting point.

3

Boundaries and comfort

Before any shoot is scheduled, we talk through the content level, wardrobe, rate, travel, and anything that is off limits — including comfort with the content being sold publicly on adult platforms. What's agreed is what gets shot — nothing more.

4

Scheduling and logistics

Once everything's agreed, we lock a date. You'll get the address, arrival timing, parking, what to bring, and travel coordination if it applies.

5

Paperwork and ID

Model release, age verification, and the required record-keeping paperwork are done before shooting starts. Nothing is rushed.

6

Pool orientation

On arrival, a walk-through of the pool, how underwater shots work, how to signal, and the stop signs we use. Water comfort first.

7

Wardrobe and content confirmation

Wardrobe for each look is confirmed, and the content scope from the booking is re-confirmed. If something has changed for you since we last spoke, the plan adjusts.

8

Shooting

Each shot is described before it happens — pose, timing, where to look, when to surface. Underwater takes are short, with surface breaks between them.

9

Breaks and check-ins

Comfort checks are built into the schedule. You can pause, skip a look, change the plan, or end early at any point. Time committed is still paid per the agreement.

10

Wrap-up, payment, next steps

Paperwork is closed out, payment is settled, and we confirm how and when imagery will be used. You leave knowing what was shot, what wasn't, and what's next.

Worth knowing

Underwater work is real work.

Underwater takes breath control, breaks, and clear communication. It can be physically tiring. The shoots are built around short takes and surface breaks so the day is doable, not exhausting. You can pause or stop at any time.
